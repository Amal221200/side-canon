## CHAPTER ONE — Failed Endeavour

### 2016

The Appalachian test facility did not look secret.

It looked old.

Concrete sunk deep into rock.

Steel blast doors bolted into the mountain face.

A launch hangar carved directly into stone.

It felt less like a lab and more like something embedded in the earth.

Inside the hangar sat the prototype.

Not a rocket.

Not quite a shuttle.

Compact. Dense. Angular.

Reinforced edges. No unnecessary curves.

The official designation was classified.

To the project files, it was the Spatial Fold Vehicle.

To Reed, it was simply:

**The Bridge.**

### Control Room

Behind layered shielding and phase-insulated glass, rows of monitors flickered to life.

Government observers.

NASA representatives.

Civilian physicists.

No soldiers.

No theatrics.

Engineers with clipboards. Analysts with data tablets.

People who understood risk.

Through the observation barrier, the vehicle rested under stark white floodlights.

Dr. Reed Richards stood at the center console.

Calm.

Precise.

Focused.

"This is a micro-fold displacement test," he said evenly.

"Not a jump. Not a traversal. A controlled compression of spatial coordinates."

No grand language. No promises.

Just math.

Behind him, Susan Storm reviewed cascading field-stability matrices, her eyes tracking harmonic tolerance margins in real time.

Ben Grimm was already strapped into the pilot's seat inside the cockpit.

Johnny Storm leaned casually against a side console, arms folded — too relaxed for someone about to step through physics.

### Inside the Vehicle

The cockpit was tight but efficient. No wasted space.

Ben adjusted his harness and glanced at the central display.

"You built something that bends space," he muttered.

"And you trust me to steer it."

Reed allowed himself a faint smirk.

"You always wanted to fly something new."

Johnny tapped the console with two fingers.

"First people to break Einstein."

Sue didn't look up from her screen.

"First people to extend him."

Reed studied them for a moment.

Childhood friend.

Brilliant partner.

Younger brother who refused to stay behind.

No speeches.

No ceremony.

Just shared history.

"Ready?"

One by one—

Ben gave a firm nod.

Sue met Reed's eyes and answered silently.

Johnny grinned.

"Let's see what's out there."

Reed turned back to the controls.

And initiated the fold.

### Initiation

The hangar doors sealed with a hydraulic thud that echoed through the mountain.

Inside the reinforced observation deck, the air felt thinner — not physically, but emotionally. Engineers leaned forward. Monitors flickered with live telemetry.

Reed's voice came over internal comms.

"Fold core charging. Field envelope initializing."

Below the mountain, the reactor housing began to hum.

Inside the cockpit, Ben's hands rested steady on the controls.

Sue watched cascading harmonic models scroll across her screen.

Johnny stared at the empty air in front of them.

Nothing happened.

Then—

The air in the hangar compressed.

Not wind.

Not force.

Pressure.

On the observation deck, a technician whispered:

"Field density climbing..."

Numbers climbed.

_88%_

_90%_

_92%_

Sue's voice remained controlled.

"Compression at ninety-two percent."

Reed adjusted a parameter with surgical precision.

"Stabilizing."

In front of the vehicle—

Space trembled.

Not opened.

Not tore.

It folded.

A subtle geometric distortion like reality inhaling.

In the control room, someone breathed:

"My God..."

The crease deepened.

For three perfect seconds—

The telemetry flattened.

Perfect.

Reed said quietly:

"Bridge established."

And then—

The vehicle vanished.

### Control Room — Reaction

Silence.

No explosion.

No debris.

Just empty air where the craft had been.

Then—

Monitors exploded with data.

"Signal lock acquired!"

"Telemetry intact!"

"Gravitational offset within predicted range!"

Applause didn't erupt — it exhaled.

Controlled excitement.

A NASA supervisor allowed himself a smile.

"They did it."

Reed's voice came through, faint but steady.

"Fold translation successful."

The room relaxed.

They thought the hardest part was over.

### The Other Side

The hangar was gone.

Stars surrounded them.

Not the way stars appear from Earth.

These were sharp. Dense. Warped.

They hovered near something massive.

A celestial body suspended in darkness.

It had no visible atmosphere.

No surface detail.

Light bent around it like water around stone.

The starfield behind it curved unnaturally.

Johnny leaned forward slowly.

"Okay..."

He let out a breath.

"That's real."

Sue stared at the lensing distortion.

"It's beautiful."

Reed's hands moved over the console.

"Gravitational lensing stable. Field cohesion holding."

Ben scanned the readouts.

"She handles fine. No drag."

For a moment—

It was pure awe.

Humanity, standing at the edge of the unknown.

Sue whispered:

"Reed... we're here."

Reed allowed himself the smallest smile.

"Yes."

### The Decision

Johnny tilted his head.

"Can we get closer?"

Reed shook it off immediately.

"No. This is observation only. We validate data and return."

Ben nodded.

"Smart."

Sue watched the energy readings.

Then she frowned.

"Reed..."

He didn't look up yet.

"Reading it."

Vacuum fluctuations began to climb.

Small at first.

Then sharper.

"Gravitational harmonics increasing," Sue said.

The celestial body did not move.

But something around it did.

Not visually.

Mathematically.

The background radiation spectrum began to spike.

Johnny leaned forward.

"That's not in the model."

Reed's fingers moved faster.

"Field compensation increasing."

Then—

The starfield behind the celestial body distorted.

Not like lensing.

Like pressure passing through fabric.

A ripple.

Far away.

But coming.

### The Storm

Sue's voice sharpened.

"Reed— that's moving."

The ripple widened.

Stars bent out of alignment as it passed.

No light.

No color.

Just the universe flexing.

Ben tightened his grip on the controls.

"That's not good."

Reed made the call instantly.

"Emergency recall. Initiate return fold."

Ben engaged the recall sequence.

Energy built around the vehicle.

The ripple accelerated.

Too fast.

Johnny stared at it.

"Reed— it's gaining."

The ripple was no longer distant.

It consumed stars silently.

A wave across existence.

"Field collapsing!" Sue shouted.

"Ben— now!"

Ben hit the return trigger.

The fold bubble began forming—

And the wave hit them.

### Impact

Everything went silent.

No sound.

No explosion.

Just distortion.

The cockpit elongated.

Compressed.

Twisted.

Metal groaned as if gravity had forgotten how to behave.

Johnny felt heat flood through his veins.

Sue felt something push outward from beneath her skin.

Reed's hands felt wrong.

Soft.

Elastic.

Ben gripped the controls—

And felt resistance inside his own bones.

Then—

Snap.

### Return

The hangar did not receive them gently.

The vehicle tore back into existence mid-air.

For half a second, it existed in two positions at once.

Then gravity chose one.

It slammed into the launch platform.

The fold bubble imploded outward.

A concussive wave rippled across the chamber.

Observation glass fractured in spiderweb patterns but held.

Engineers were thrown backward.

Monitors shorted.

The lights cut out.

Then emergency red flooded the room.

Someone shouted:

"Containment breach?"

"No— field collapse only!"

Seismic monitors lit up across the wall.

Deep-harmonic sensors registered a shock pulse traveling outward through bedrock.

One technician stared at the cascading data.

"That's propagating..."

The mountain answered with a low, distant rumble.

### Appalachia — Monitoring Station

Miles away.

In a remote research shaft carved into ancient rock, Dr. Harvey Elder leaned toward his screens.

Readings he had been tracking for months suddenly spiked.

Not surface tremors.

Deep resonance.

Lithospheric displacement.

He whispered to himself:

"That's impossible."

The waveform matched something he had once theorized.

A harmonic disturbance not native to tectonic movement.

The floor beneath him shifted.

Dust rained from the ceiling.

The central support column fractured.

Elder grabbed the console.

"Stabilize—"

The shaft groaned.

Then the ground gave way.

The platform beneath him dropped.

Rock swallowed the chamber in cascading collapse.

He fell into darkness.

Above him, stone sealed.

Silence reclaimed the mountain.

### Inside the Cockpit

Back in the hangar, alarms screamed.

The cockpit lighting flickered between red and black.

Smoke hissed from ruptured conduits.

Reed's ears rang.

He tried to focus on the instrument panel.

Half of it was dead.

"We're back," Sue said softly.

Johnny laughed once — short and breathless.

"Yeah... we're back."

He flexed his fingers.

Heat shimmered faintly around them.

He frowned.

"Why do I feel like I just ran a marathon?"

Sue reached for the console.

Her hand passed through the light at a strange angle.

For a fraction of a second—

It wasn't there.

She pulled it back quickly.

Her pulse jumped.

Reed lifted his hand toward a cracked display.

His fingers extended farther than they should have.

Just a few centimeters.

But they didn't stop where they were supposed to.

He jerked his hand back.

Stared at it.

Breathing shallow.

Behind him—

A sound.

Stone grinding against stone.

"Reed..."

The voice was deeper.

Thicker.

They turned.

Ben was pushing himself upright from the pilot's seat.

His flight suit strained.

Skin along his forearms hardened.

Fine cracks spread outward like fault lines.

"Reed... what happened to me?"

His knuckles thickened.

Fingers stiffened.

Color drained.

Gray bled through flesh.

Sue took a step forward.

"Ben, stay still—"

His jaw tightened.

Cheekbones shifted.

Veins rose and fossilized beneath the surface.

Fragments fell from his arms and struck the floor with heavy clicks.

Johnny stepped back instinctively.

"Ben—"

Ben looked down.

His hands were no longer hands.

They were layered stone.

Solid.

Unyielding.

He flexed them once.

The cockpit frame groaned under the force.

Outside, engineers were shouting.

Containment teams running toward the vehicle.

Inside—

Silence.

Reed stepped closer.

Slowly.

His voice barely worked.

"I'll fix it."

Ben looked at him.

Not angry.

Not yet.

Just confused.

And that was worse.

For the first time in his life—

Reed Richards had no equation.

## CHAPTER TWO — Separate Orbits

### 2022

Morning light spilled across Manhattan glass.

The Baxter Building did not resemble a superhero headquarters.

It resembled a think tank that never slept.

Inside, elevators moved constantly. Labs hummed. Screens flickered with simulations mid-calculation. Whiteboards were half-erased, equations layered over older equations.

Scientists argued in low voices over coffee.

Engineers rolled prototype housings across polished floors.

The building felt alive.

Not flashy.

Alive.

### Reed Richards

A lecture hall filled with graduate students and visiting physicists.

Reed stood before a projection of spacetime curvature models rotating slowly behind him.

"No equation survives first contact with reality," he said evenly. "That is not failure. That is refinement."

A hand went up.

"Dr. Richards, do you believe spatial folding is commercially viable within our lifetime?"

A pause.

He considered the room.

"Commercial viability," he replied carefully, "is not the correct benchmark. Stability is."

No smile. No bravado.

Afterward, he stepped into a glass-walled lab overlooking the city.

Inside, a team waited.

"Vacuum fluctuation drift is still present," one researcher said.

Reed leaned over the console. Adjusted three parameters.

"Compensate at the harmonic source, not the output."

The model stabilized.

The room relaxed.

He moved through the lab like someone who lived inside equations.

Later, alone, he stood before a sealed chamber.

A faint distortion shimmered in the air.

He stepped through a localized micro-fold and reappeared across the room.

Precise.

Contained.

Efficient.

No one applauded.

It was routine.

### Susan Storm

The chamber was quieter than the lab, but heavier.

Flags lined the back wall.

Cameras hovered.

Susan stood behind a podium engraved with an international scientific council emblem.

"Emerging technologies must not outpace ethical oversight," she said calmly. "Innovation without accountability destabilizes more than markets."

A journalist raised a hand.

"Dr. Storm, does that apply to private research institutions as well?"

She met the question directly.

"It applies especially to them."

After the session, aides approached with briefings and travel schedules.

She listened. Delegated. Corrected.

In a private corridor, she paused alone.

For a fraction of a second, her hand shimmered— light bending across invisible geometry.

She steadied it.

Controlled.

She opened her phone.

Texted Johnny.

_Saturday. Rooftop. Don't tell him._

Then Ben.

_You in?_

She exhaled.

Reed never remembered.

She never reminded him.

### Johnny Storm

Heat distortion rippled across the Nevada sky.

An experimental aerospace prototype tore through atmosphere at Mach velocity.

A streak of flame kept pace beside it.

Not wild.

Measured.

Johnny cut ignition and dropped beside the landing strip, boots skidding across hot asphalt.

Engineers applauded.

A production crew stood waiting with cameras.

One of them called out:

"Human Torch, over here!"

He flashed a grin, pulling off tinted flight lenses.

The name had stuck years ago.

Media coined it after a high-altitude rescue.

It became branding.

Endorsements.

Contracts.

Later, inside a sleek office overlooking the Strip, an agent slid a folder across the table.

"Energy drink campaign. Global rollout. Seven figures."

Johnny flipped through the pages.

"Weekend shoot?"

"Saturday."

Johnny closed the folder.

"Can't. I'm busy."

"Busy doing what?"

He didn't answer.

His phone buzzed.

Sue.

He smirked.

_Wouldn't miss it._

That night, alone in his apartment, he wasn't looking at himself on magazine covers.

He was studying propulsion diagrams on a tablet.

His flames flickered softly in the dark.

Controlled.

Intentional.

### Ben Grimm

NASA training facility.

Steel and silence.

A new astronaut class stood in formation.

Ben paced before them.

"Reentry doesn't care about your confidence," he said, voice like stone grinding. "It cares about angles."

He tapped the hull of a training module.

It dented slightly.

He ignored it.

A trainee asked, "Sir, what's the most important thing in orbit?"

Ben didn't hesitate.

"Trust your team."

Later, in the observation room, he watched a test rocket ignite.

Engines roared.

The rocket climbed.

He stood still.

Not longing.

Not regret.

Just watching.

His phone buzzed.

Sue.

He read it.

Then typed:

_Wouldn't miss it._

### Late Night — Baxter Building

The building had quieted.

Only low mechanical hums remained.

Reed stood alone in his private lab.

On the central display:

Archived fold incident data.

Storm energy signatures.

Vacuum fluctuation spikes.

Cellular resonance mapping.

He initiated a new simulation.

If molecular constants could be realigned—

If boundary exposure effects could be reversed—

Equations cascaded across the screen.

He adjusted parameters.

Ran another model.

Every scenario terminated in failure.

Ben's physiology remained the outlier.

High-density crystalline stabilization integrated at cellular level.

Reversal resulted in catastrophic structural collapse.

Fatal in 97.4% of simulations.

He ran it again anyway.

Different variables.

Different thresholds.

Failure.

The room felt smaller.

He stared at Ben's structural scans.

"I'll fix it," he murmured to no one.

His phone buzzed.

A message from Susan:

_Can you come by Saturday evening? I'd like to see you._

He read it once.

Did not notice the date.

He typed back:

_Of course._

Outside the windows, Manhattan lights shimmered.

His screen dimmed slightly as the system auto-saved another failed attempt.

He shut down the simulation.

But not the data.

He never shut down the data.

## CHAPTER THREE — The Rooftop

The elevator doors opened to the upper terrace of the Baxter Building.

Reed stepped out adjusting his cufflinks.

Dark suit. Crisp shirt. Polished shoes.

Not extravagant — but intentional.

He had assumed dinner.

A quiet conversation.

Perhaps something overdue.

The terrace was dimly lit by subtle overhead fixtures. The city stretched endlessly beyond the railing — Manhattan glowing in warm gold and white.

For half a second, he thought he was early.

Then—

"About time, Stretch."

Reed stopped.

Johnny leaned against the railing, arms folded, grinning.

Ben stood beside a long minimal table, hands in the pockets of a tailored jacket that somehow fit around stone shoulders.

Sue stood a few steps back.

Holding a small cake.

A single candle shaped like the number 40 burned steadily in the wind.

Reed blinked.

He hadn't noticed.

Johnny pushed off the railing, eyeing Reed's suit.

"Wow. You think this was a board meeting?"

Ben stepped forward and wrapped him in a firm, careful hug.

Stone against fabric.

"Forty, pal," Ben said. "You're not getting younger."

Reed looked at the candle again.

"Forty?"

Sue smiled — not teasing. Warm.

"You forgot."

He searched his memory.

Ran through the week.

The calendar.

The date.

He exhaled.

"I was running simulations."

Johnny snorted.

"Of course you were."

Sue stepped closer.

The wind caught her hair slightly.

"We thought you deserved a night off."

Reed looked at each of them.

Ben.

Johnny.

Sue.

All here.

All steady.

All... fine.

Something in his expression shifted.

Not embarrassment.

Not sadness.

Something softer.

"I thought this was dinner," he admitted quietly.

Johnny's grin widened.

"Oh it is. Just not the kind you expected."

Ben clapped him once on the shoulder.

"Relax. We didn't invite the press."

Reed glanced out at the skyline.

Lights shimmering below.

For the first time in a long time—

He wasn't looking at data.

He wasn't calculating.

He wasn't fixing.

He was just standing there.

With them.

Sue held the cake toward him.

"Make a wish."

He hesitated.

Johnny leaned in.

"If you say ‘stable harmonic resonance,' I'm pushing you off the roof."

Ben chuckled.

Reed allowed himself a small smile.

Closed his eyes.

For once—

He didn't calculate the probability of anything.

He blew out the candle.

### After Dinner

Dinner plates were pushed aside.

The skyline shimmered beyond the terrace railing.

Wind moved between buildings. Sirens far below blended into the city's constant pulse.

Johnny leaned back in his chair, a bottle of beer balanced loosely in his hand.

"Six years," he said. "Feels like yesterday we were bending space."

Ben exhaled lightly.

"Yeah. And yesterday you were setting lab equipment on fire."

"That was controlled ignition," Johnny corrected.

Sue smiled.

"Six years," she repeated softly.

Ben took a slow drink.

"At NASA they still ask me what happened," he said. "Some folks think I'm an X-Man."

Johnny grinned.

"You'd look good in yellow spandex."

Ben rolled his eyes.

"Not happening."

He shrugged.

"Truth is... most people don't stare much anymore. They get used to it. I train astronauts now. Structural stress, reentry simulation, team dynamics."

"Trust your team," Johnny said.

Ben nodded.

"Exactly."

Sue leaned back slightly.

"At least you're still sending people to space."

Johnny raised a brow.

"What about you, Madam Geneva?"

Sue shot him a look.

"I'm not anyone's Madam."

But she answered anyway.

"I've been splitting time between New York and Geneva. UN science advisory board. Environmental stabilization initiatives. Enhanced individual policy integration."

Johnny blinked.

"That sounds way less fun than flying."

"It's necessary," she replied calmly.

Reed had been listening quietly.

"I saw your testimony last month," he said.

Sue turned toward him.

"You did?"

There was something unguarded in that question.

Reed nodded.

"The climate-resilience framework proposal. The boundary regulation clause you added was smart. You prevented three governments from bypassing oversight."

A small pause.

Sue's expression softened.

"Really?"

He met her eyes.

"Yes."

It was simple.

But it mattered.

Johnny leaned back, smirking.

"Look at that. Reed watches the news."

Ben chuckled.

Johnny took a sip of beer.

"Anyway, having powers has advantages."

Sue raised an eyebrow.

"Oh yes. We've seen the magazines. You're very committed to international cooperation."

Johnny groaned.

"I respect women."

Ben smirked.

"Sure. That's why you commit to at least a month."

Laughter cut through the air.

Then Sue turned to Reed.

"And you?" she asked gently. "Have you discovered light-speed travel yet?"

Johnny leaned forward.

"Or built a time machine?"

Reed stared at the city lights.

"I've been safe," he said quietly. "Teaching. Refining. Working with colleagues. Focusing on ethics. On consequence."

A beat.

"But I haven't stopped thinking about that night."

The laughter faded.

Wind moved across the terrace.

"I ran simulations," Reed continued. "Over and over. Using every fragment of storm data we captured. I tried recalibrating molecular constants. Reversing structural integration."

He looked at Ben.

"I can't."

Ben's jaw tightened slightly.

"Reed."

"It's not that I won't," Reed said. "It's that I can't."

He exhaled.

"I stole your life. Your future. Your dreams."

Ben set his bottle down.

"Hey."

Reed didn't look at him.

"I can't live knowing I did that."

Silence settled heavier than before.

Ben leaned forward.

"My life isn't as rocky as my body," he said calmly.

Johnny blinked.

Sue's attention sharpened.

Ben continued.

"I have someone."

Johnny sat upright.

"Hold on."

Ben's voice softened.

"Her name's Alicia."

The wind carried the city noise faintly upward.

"She can't see. She doesn't care what I look like," Ben said. "We found comfort in each other. And it turned into something more."

Johnny grinned slowly.

"Ben Grimm has a girlfriend."

Sue smiled warmly.

"That's wonderful."

Reed nodded.

"I'm happy for you."

Ben studied him carefully.

"What about you, Stretch?"

Reed's eyes lifted.

For a fraction of a second—

He looked at Sue.

Then away.

"I didn't have time for that."

Ben caught the glance.

Understood it.

Johnny, oblivious, turned to Sue.

"And you? You're not seeing anyone either, are you?"

Sue gave him a warning look.

"Johnny."

He held up his hands.

"I'm just asking."

Reed didn't speak.

But something in him shifted.

Not hope.

Not yet.

Possibility.

Then—

A sound.

Not loud.

Deep.

Like a distant thunder beneath the city.

They all paused.

Another vibration.

Stronger.

The terrace lights flickered.

Ben stood first.

"What was that?"

Across the skyline—

A section of street several blocks away dropped.

Not collapsed.

Dropped.

As if the earth had inhaled.

Concrete cracked outward in jagged lines.

Cars tilted and slid.

A building along the edge of the fracture leaned violently.

People screamed — faint but rising.

Another tremor rippled through the skyline.

A second section of ground caved inward.

Sue stepped forward to the railing.

"What is happening?"

Johnny's body ignited reflexively, flames rolling along his arms.

Ben's hands clenched.

Reed didn't move.

He stared.

Not at the destruction.

At the pattern.

The cracks weren't random.

They were aligned.

Resonant.

Structured.

His face drained.

"It's harmonic."

The ground below them trembled again.

And Manhattan began to sink.

### Response

The skyline fractured again.

Concrete tore downward.

A subway entrance vanished into a widening seam.

Screams rose from below, faint but multiplying.

Wind whipped across the terrace.

For half a second, they all stood still.

Then instinct replaced hesitation.

Johnny didn't wait.

Flame erupted from his skin in a controlled surge.

His outer jacket ignited instantly — ash scattering into the wind.

Beneath it, a reinforced flight suit shimmered into view, heat-resistant fabric designed for this exact state.

He hovered a foot above the terrace.

"Ben."

Ben didn't need more.

Johnny grabbed him at the shoulder.

Flame enveloped them both — not burning stone, not touching him — and they shot into the sky toward the collapsing district.

Sue stepped toward the railing.

"Reed—"

He was already removing his jacket.

Underneath, a tailored polymer suit flexed subtly as he moved.

Engineered for molecular elasticity.

No hesitation this time.

He looked at her once.

Not calculating.

Trusting.

"Hold on."

Sue doesn't hesitate.

She stepped into his arms.

They jumped.

The wind swallowed them.

Midair, Reed's body expanded outward.

Arms widening.

Torso flattening.

Surface tension redistributing.

He became a living canopy.

A controlled aerodynamic membrane catching the air.

They descended fast but stable.

Sue adjusted her center of gravity instinctively.

They landed near the first fracture.

The street had collapsed inward like a sinkhole from hell.

A bus hung half-suspended over the edge.

Ben hit the pavement with enough force to crack it.

Johnny circled overhead, flame lighting the dust-filled air.

"Bus first!" Johnny shouted.

Ben grabbed the rear axle as the front wheels slipped.

The metal screamed.

He planted himself and held.

"Move!" he roared at the passengers frozen inside.

Johnny darted down, heat flaring.

He sliced through jammed door hinges with precision bursts.

Not wild flame.

Surgical.

Passengers stumbled out.

Reed extended across the fracture, anchoring himself to a lamppost on one side and a streetlight on the other.

His torso stretched into a living bridge.

"Single file!" he shouted.

They ran across him.

Sue's hands lifted.

Invisible force expanded outward like a second atmosphere.

A building façade above them cracked loose.

Bricks fell—

And stopped midair against her field.

She redirected the debris sideways, guiding it into an already-collapsed zone to prevent secondary impact.

Another tremor hit.

A fire hydrant erupted.

Water blasted across the street.

Johnny vaporized the worst of it instantly to prevent flooding into the fissure.

Ben pulled the bus fully back onto stable ground and shoved it aside.

"Street's not stable!" Johnny warned from above.

Reed scanned the fracture pattern even as he held position.

"It's harmonic, repeating intervals. The collapse is spreading radially."

Sue's jaw tightened.

"Then we stabilize radially."

She expanded her field beneath a sagging corner building, distributing the load long enough for evacuation crews to pull civilians clear.

Sirens grew louder now.

Police.

Fire.

Chaos.

Then—

A blur of movement down the avenue.

Figures landing in coordinated precision.

The New X-Men.

They moved without hesitation.

One froze a widening crack with controlled cryokinesis.

Another telekinetically lifted trapped vehicles.

A speedster evacuated civilians block by block.

The coordination was immediate.

Professional.

Johnny hovered midair, flames reflecting off shattered glass.

"Well," he muttered, "backup."

Ben exhaled once.

Reed retracted back to human form beside Sue.

They stood shoulder to shoulder.

Watching the team take over large-scale stabilization.

For the first time in six years—

They weren't hiding.

They weren't alone.

They weren't fractured.

They were standing in the open, in full view of the city.

Together.

The skyline still trembled.

Dust clouded the night.

Somewhere deep below Manhattan—

Something was moving.

Reed stared at the fracture lines carving across the streets.

Not random.

Structured.

Intentional.

And this time—

He didn't hesitate.

"Let's find the source."

## CHAPTER FOUR — Fault Lines

Morning light filtered through the upper floors of the Baxter Building.

News feeds looped silently across wall-mounted screens.

**MANHATTAN SINKHOLE EVENT**

**UNPRECEDENTED URBAN COLLAPSE**

**ENHANCED INDIVIDUALS ASSIST IN RESCUE**

Reed stood at the center of the main lab.

Holographic projections hovered midair — fracture maps, seismic wave patterns, harmonic overlays.

Sue leaned against the console.

Ben stood near a reinforced table, arms crossed.

Johnny paced.

On the display:

Manhattan's street grid.

Overlaid with red lines.

Perfect radial symmetry.

"This wasn't tectonic," Reed said.

"It was induced."

Johnny stopped pacing.

"Induced how?"

Reed adjusted the model.

Waveforms pulsed outward from a central convergence point beneath Lower Manhattan.
