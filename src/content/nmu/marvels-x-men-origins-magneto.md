## Prelude: Vornavia 1970's

Vornavia was a country that learned to survive by pretending.

It sat in the middle of Europe, pressed between borders that had been redrawn so many times no one trusted the maps anymore. The Baltic winds carried salt and cold across its industrial coast, rusting shipyards that no longer built ships and factories that no longer remembered what they had been for.

After the war, the flags changed.

The uniforms changed.

The language of power changed.

The fear did not.

The new government called it _reconstruction_. Unity. Order. Healing. They spoke endlessly about rebuilding the nation from the ashes of occupation, about loyalty, vigilance, and shared sacrifice. Posters showed smiling workers, clasped hands, bright futures.

But some neighborhoods never made it into the posters.

The old Jewish quarters still stood; narrow streets and heavy doors, synagogues spared by bureaucracy more than mercy. The buildings were renamed _working-class housing_, as if changing the words could erase the history clinging to the walls.

Synagogues were permitted to exist.

Rabbis were permitted to preach.

They were also watched.

Every sermon had listeners who did not pray.

Every business application had questions that did not need answers.

Licenses took longer. Approvals were delayed. Permits were "lost." No one ever said _Jew_ aloud in official documents. They said _foreign influence_. _Unreliable background_. _Unclear loyalties_.

In the streets, the words were cruder.

Not shouted. Not violent.

Just sharp enough to remind you where you stood.

A joke muttered under breath.

A glance that lingered too long.

A nickname that everyone pretended not to hear.

The Roma lived on the edges of cities that refused to claim them. Refugees from other collapsed borders drifted through Vornavia like ghosts, always temporary, always suspect. Everyone was someone else's problem.

Paranoia was the real currency.

Neighbors learned not to ask questions. Children learned which names are not to mention in school. Families learned that safety came from quiet, not justice.

And still, people lived.

They worked in factories that paid little and demanded silence. They stood in bread lines and told themselves it was better than hunger. They married, argued, and celebrated holidays behind drawn curtains. They learned to fold themselves small.

Vornavia did not hate it openly anymore.

It humiliated efficiently.

And in one of those old quarters; where history had been painted over but never erased, a boy was growing up learning the most dangerous lesson of all:

That survival was possible.

That dignity was optional.

And that if you waited long enough, the world would decide who you were allowed to be.

Vornavia did not know it yet.

But it was raising a child who would remember everything it tried so hard to forget.

## Chapter 1: Dark Past

### Childhood: Erik's Home, Vornavia

Erik Magnus Lehnsherr was a Jewish boy, gifted with academic intelligence, born to a lower-class Jewish family in the heart of **Vornavia**, a cold industrial nation still nursing its wounds from the war. It was 1970, but in Vornavia, time felt trapped between eras; the ruins of old battles standing beside the walls of new factories.

Erik lived with his father, mother, and elder sister in a cramped ghetto apartment. His sister worked long hours at small jobs so they could afford Erik's education, their one fragile hope for a better life. But Erik despised school. Not because he lacked curiosity, but because every day there felt like a punishment. His classmates mocked him for his name, his accent, and his faith. Even the teachers seemed to carry quiet contempt in their eyes, always finding a reason to humiliate him; a wrong answer, a late paper, a missing uniform button.

When he complained, his father would place a hand on his shoulder and say:

"Listen, Erik... people are misguided. They don't know what they're doing. They're still living in the past. But that will change; it must. Don't throw your life away because of their ignorance. Education is the only power a man can claim for himself... and you, my son, have that power."

Erik never argued with him again after that. He would simply nod and join his family for dinner, pretending for a moment that life was normal.

### Few Days Later

One afternoon, school had just ended. Erik stepped out into the street, clutching his worn bag, when a deafening **explosion** shattered the air.

The world spun. He fell to the ground, dazed, ears ringing. When he lifted his head, everything was chaos, the air thick with dust and fire. Screams. Gunfire. Sirens.

It wasn't war... but it looked like one.  
A group of **Neo-Nazi extremists**, masked and armed, moved through the streets like a swarm, shouting about "purity" and "cleansing." They claimed to be reclaiming the nation from the _lower races;_ words that made no sense to a child yet carried the same venom that haunted his family for generations.

Erik ran, heart pounding, lungs burning, through alleys and broken streets, desperate to reach the ghetto. As he ran, he saw things no child should: neighbors executed in doorways, houses set ablaze, the same banners that once hung in history books now carried again by men who never learned.

By the time he reached home, the block was in ruins. His small apartment was nothing but smoke and rubble. He screamed his family's names as he clawed through the debris with bloodied hands.

And then he saw them.  
His mother and sister; burnt, locked in each other's final embrace.

A sound escaped him, half breath, half scream.

Then, from behind, a voice.  
His father. Alive, staggering, calling his name. But before Erik could reach him, a soldier lifted his rifle and fired.

Erik froze. The world slowed. The echo of the gunshot seemed to stretch forever. His father fell to the ground, lifeless.

Erik dropped down to his knees beside him, trembling. He buried his face against his father's chest, sobbing, desperate, and helpless. The smoke stung his eyes, but the pain in his chest burned hotter.

Through his tears, he saw his mother's hand lying just a few feet away, charred but recognizable, the same hand that used to fix his hair every morning before school.

Erik's cries turned into short, broken gasps. His lips quivered. His eyes, once filled with innocence, began to harden.

Footsteps echoed behind him.  
The soldiers were approaching, shouting orders in their native tongue. One of them laughed. Another muttered, "Leave the rat for the camps."

Erik's tears stopped. His breathing slowed. Something shifted in his face, a quiet, deadly stillness.

As the soldiers moved closer, planning to take him, the air began to vibrate.  
Loose nails, bullet casings, bits of metal, they all began to **tremble**.

The ground hummed beneath his feet.  
The soldier shouted, "Kill the boy!"

And then,

A scream tore from Erik's throat, raw and furious.  
A storm of metal erupted outward, bullets twisting midair, knives ripping from belts, rifles bending like paper. The men were thrown, crushed, impaled by the very weapons they carried.

When it ended, silence. Only smoke and corpses.

Erik's body went limp. He collapsed beside his father's remains, his small chest rising and falling in exhaustion, until even that stopped.

The boy who once dreamed of a peaceful life was gone.

### Lab in a remote island

When Erik awoke, he was no longer in Vornavia.  
Cold white lights burned above him, and the sound of waves echoed faintly through the concrete. Through a small, barred window he could see an endless horizon of water. Later he would learn this was **Falcon Island**, a secret research base off the coast of Malta. The Vornavian military had handed him over to scientists who studied "genetic abnormalities."

They told him he was _lucky to be alive._  
He did not feel lucky.

For the next five years, Erik lived inside a metal-lined cell no larger than a train compartment. The walls were made of an alloy that blocked magnetic fields; even the air vents were ceramic. Whenever he tried to concentrate, the hum of generators drowned out the faint pulse of metal he could still sense in the world beyond.

The experiments were endless:

- **Neurological mapping.** Electrodes attached to his scalp while currents pulsed through him to measure the brain's reaction.
- **Stress induction.** They flooded his chamber with noise or cold to see when his powers would flare.
- **Isolation trials.** Days without light or human voices, to test the limits of endurance.
- **Cellular sampling.** Needles drawing blood to be compared with normal human DNA.

Each test had the same conclusion: _"Subject E-7 remains unstable."_

Over time, Erik learned to stop screaming. He listened instead to the tiny clicks inside machines, to the hidden rhythm of magnets in the walls. He began to _understand_ his cage, not just fearing it. The pain forged discipline. The loneliness turned into focus.

Among the staff was **Dr. Keller**, the only one who spoke to him kindly. "You're proof of what humanity can become," she said once. "But they'll never let you walk free." Her words stayed with him.

### The Night of Escape

Five years later, during a violent Mediterranean storm, the facility's power grid failed. The lights flickered, alarms stuttered, and for the first time since his capture, Erik felt the full thrum of the island metal around him.

He stood still, rainwater leaking through cracks in the ceiling, electricity fading. Then the locks were disengaged.

What followed was chaos, screams, the crash of bending steel; corridors collapsing as magnetic waves tore through the complex. By dawn, Falcon Island was silent.

Erik stepped outside into the rain. He was barefoot, soaked in blood; his torn hospital garments clinging to his skin. Lightning flashed over the sea, illuminating the ruins behind him. The rain poured down, washing away the stains of everything that had happened inside.

He looked toward the horizon; the direction of the mainland.  
For a long moment, he simply breathed.

The boy who had once begged for his father's life was gone.  
What remained was a 15-year-old forged in captivity, with the certainty that the world would never again cage him.

## Chapter 2: Adaptation

12 years after Falcon Island vanished into the sea, **Erik Lehnsherr** no longer existed on any official record.

In his place was **Dr. Magnus;** a visiting genetic specialist attached to a multinational humanitarian research initiative operating somewhere along Europe's northern coast. The camp did not appear on tourist maps. It existed in a grey zone: funded jointly by several governments, staffed by private contractors, and protected by a mandate that allowed _limited autonomy_ in the name of post-conflict rehabilitation.

On paper, it was a refugee medical center.

In practice, it was a sorting ground.

Rows of temporary housing stretched across a windswept plain, fenced but not imprisoned, guarded but not militarized. Families displaced by wars, sanctions, and collapsed states lived here while being "evaluated." Medical tents stood beside prefabricated laboratories. Surveillance towers overlooked everything, not aggressively, but persistently.

Erik arrived at the dusk.

He wore a simple coat, carried a single case, and walked with the quiet confidence of someone who had learned long ago how to disappear in plain sight. His credentials passed inspection without difficulty. His doctorate, _Applied Genetic Pathology,_ was real enough to survive scrutiny. Years of obsessive study, stolen research, and brutal self-discipline had ensured that.

He had not come for the refugees.

He had come for what lay beneath them.

### **The Camp**

By evening, Erik was assigned a small quarter near the outer medical wing. He declined dinner, citing fatigue. Once alone, he unfolded a hand-drawn map across the narrow desk.

The map was not of the camp as presented.

It showed power conduits.

Hidden elevator shafts.

Unmarked supply routes.

Metal density anomalies beneath the soil.

There were places where the earth hummed differently.

That was where they were hiding them.

Erik traced the lines with his finger, memorizing patrol timings. Guards rotated every forty minutes. The northern perimeter relied on outdated magnetic locks, cheap, and overconfident. He had already tested them subtly, nudging the fields just enough to confirm resistance thresholds.

He was calm.

He always was, before violence.

A voice broke the silence.

"You know, most doctors don't look at maps like they're planning an invasion."

Erik stiffened.

He turned slowly.

The man standing in the doorway was about his age, mid-twenties, perhaps a little older. Bald. Well-dressed, but casually so. His posture was relaxed; his expression openly curious rather than suspicious.

"I'm sorry," the man added, raising his hands slightly. "I was looking for a medical coordinator. Wrong room."

Erik closed the map.

"You found it," he said flatly. "Now leave."

The man smiled, unfazed.

"That's a shame. I was hoping to make a friend on my first night." He stepped back, offering a hand. "Charles Xavier."

Erik didn't take it.

"I'm tired," Erik said. "Good night."

Charles studied for a moment longer, not with his eyes alone. Something unreadable passed behind his calm expression. Then he nodded.

"Of course. Sleep well, Doctor."

He left without another word.

Erik locked the door.

For the first time that evening, his breathing changed.

### **Midnight**

The camp slept lightly. Floodlights dimmed but never shut off. Generators hummed beneath the soil like distant thunder.

Erik moved silently between structures, staying within shadowed corridors formed by supply containers and fencing. No alarms sounded as he passed. The magnetic locks disengaged at his touch; their mechanisms confused rather than broken.

He followed two guards toward a maintenance building marked with an innocuous symbol, water processing.

The elevator hidden inside descended far deeper than any plumbing required.

Erik waited until the doors closed behind them, then stepped into the shaft, riding the metal frame downward as if gravity had made an exception for him.

Below, the air was colder.

Sterile.

The facility was compact but efficient. Cells lined in a central corridor, reinforced with non-magnetic composites. Inside them were people, young, frightened, restrained not by chains but by design.

Mutants.

One of them looked up as Erik approached.

Blue fur. Enlarged hands. Intelligent eyes filled with disbelief.

**Hank McCoy**

In the next cell, a girl watched silently, her form flickering for just a fraction of a second, face shifting, then returning.

**Raven** **Darkhölme**

Erik reached for the lock.

Gunmetal clicked behind him.

"Hands where we can see them."

Four guards. Rifles raised. Nervous. Untrained for what stood in front of them.

Erik didn't turn.

He felt the metal in their weapons. The screws in the walls. The wiring in the ceiling. The moment stretched, long enough for him to decide how many would die first.

He waited.

Then the moment vanished.

The guards collapsed where they stood, weapons clattering harmlessly to the floor.

Erik spun around.

Charles stood at the end of the corridor, breathing steadily, eyes focused but calm.

"We don't have much time," Charles said. "More will come."

For a heartbeat, Erik simply just stared.

Then he tore the locks open.

### **Escape**

They moved quickly. Efficiently. Not as strangers, but as two men who understood urgency.

Cells opened. Mutants stumbled free, confused, terrified, barely believing what was happening. Erik shielded them instinctively, bending steel doors into barriers as alarms finally screamed to life.

They surfaced miles away from the camp, beyond the perimeter of sensors, near a jagged stretch of coastline battered by waves and wind.

The sea in front of them was empty.

Then it wasn't.

Water parted silently as a sleek; angular vessel rose just enough to reveal a hatch. Its surface absorbed light unnaturally, contours shifting as cloaking systems disengaged.

High-tech. Purpose-built. Hidden in plain sight.

Erik paused.

This was not something acquired casually.

Erik turns to Charles

"Who are you?" He asked.

Charles smiled faintly.

"Someone who's been looking for people like you for a very long time."

The mutants boarded and the hatch sealed behind them; the vessel submerged smoothly, engines whisper-quiet.

The vessel angled westward, disappearing beneath the dark sea, toward a continent neither of them yet called home.

## Chapter 3: Beginnings

The submarine cut through the Atlantic in complete silence, its cloaking field absorbing sound and light alike. Inside, the cabin was dim, illuminated by soft instrument panels and recessed strips along the floor. No restraints. No alarms. Just the steady hum of motion beneath the sea.

For a while, no one spoke.

Most of the rescued mutants sat where they had first settled, too tired to move, too alert to sleep. Freedom, it turned out, was disorienting when you didn't trust it yet.

**Erik Lehnsherr** stood near the forward bulkhead; eyes fixed on a schematic scrolling across one of the displays. He could feel the vessel as much as see it, the alloy composition, the precise calibration of its systems, the intentional restraint in its design.

This wasn't any stolen technology.

"You were already here," Erik said finally, without turning. "At the camp. Planning the rescue."

Behind him, **Charles Xavier** sat calmly, hands resting on the armrests of his chair.

"You are not the only one who wants to save our kind," Charles replied. "I've been planning an extraction for several days. Watching how the camp operated. Following the money. Identifying who was in charge."

Erik glanced back, skeptical.

"I sent one of my colleagues ahead of time," Charles continued. "As a prisoner."

Erik's eyes shifted to the blue-furred man seated nearby.

"Dr. Hank McCoy," Charles said, a faint smile touching his lips. "Geneticist, engineer, architect, and partner in crime."

Hank lifted his hand awkwardly. "I volunteered. They talk more freely when they think you're powerless."

"You let them cage you," Erik said.

Hank shrugged. "I wanted to about the environment inside the change and the prisoners so I can share it to Charles."

That answer seemed to be a bit odd.

Charles inclined his head. "Let me re-introduce myself. Charles Xavier, an Oxford Graduate. I'm a geneticist by training. Neurology as well." A beat. "I'm also a telepath."

The air shifted subtly at that admission, unease, curiosity, guarded thoughts.

"I don't read minds without permission," Charles added calmly. "Especially not here."

That seemed to help.

Erik exhaled slowly. "My name isn't just Magnus," he said. "It's Erik Magnus Lehnsherr."

A few heads turned.

"I can manipulate magnetic fields," Erik went on. "Metal. Energy. Infrastructure. It's… broad." A pause. "I'm looking for someone. Or rather, for the people responsible for what was done to me."

He didn't explain it further.

No one asked him to.

One by one, others began to speak, not because they were prompted, but because silence no longer felt safe.

A young man near the center leaned forward, rubbing his hands together.

"Calvin Rankin," he said. "People call me Mimic." He hesitated. "I… pick things up. From others. Strength. Reflexes. Sometimes more than that." His jaw tightened. "I don't always know what's mine anymore."

Charles nodded slowly. Erik watched him with open caution.

Against the far wall, a massive man shifted his weight.

"Fred Dukes," he said. "Blob." His voice was rough and tiring. "They locked me up because I couldn't be hidden. That's all." He shrugged. "I don't have plans. Just don't want another cage."

Nearby, a well-dressed man spoke next, voice calm, almost pleasant.

"Jason Wyngarde." A faint smile. "I influence perception. Illusions. Thoughts." He glanced around the cabin. "They were very interested in that."

Erik's eyes lingered on him for a moment longer than necessary.

In the corner, someone shifted uneasily.

His face… wasn't consistent.

"I-" The man stopped. Swallowed. "Morph," he said eventually, as if choosing the word carefully. "I change. More than just how I look." His hands twitched faintly. "I don't always remember who I was before they started."

Raven watched him quietly, saying nothing.

Finally, a low voice came from the reinforced section of the hull.

"Victor Creed."

Everyone felt it when he spoke.

"I don't want anything from any of you," **Victor Creed** continued. "They locked me up because they were scared." A grin, sharp and humorless. "They were right."

Erik stiffened. Charles raised his hand gently, not as a command, but as a reminder.

The tension eased, but it didn't vanish.

A few others spoke after that; names less memorable, powers less defined. A woman who could bend light. A man whose skin hardened when he panicked. Someone who hadn't figured out what made them different yet, only that it terrified the doctors.

They didn't stay long on the subject.

Eventually, Charles spoke again.

"We're heading to my home in New York," he said. "It's safe. Private. Temporary, if you want it to be. No obligations."

"And after that?" Someone asked.

Charles looked around the room before answering. "After that, you decide what comes next."

The submarine continued west, carrying them toward land.

Toward choices.

Some of them already knew they wouldn't stay.

But for now, that decision could wait.

### Xavier's Property

The gates opened without a ceremony.

The road curved inward through old trees and open lawn, the mansion revealing itself slowly, like it wasn't trying to impress anyone. Stone, ivy, wide windows catching the late light. Gardens laid out with patience rather than symmetry.

A few of them stopped walking.

One of the younger mutants laughed under his breath. "This is real?"

Someone else reached down and pressed their palm into the grass, as if expecting it to disappear.

**Calvin Rankin** stayed close to the others, shoulders tight. The closer they got to the house, the more unsettled he looked. Too many people. Too many presences. He rubbed his arms like he was cold.

**Fred Dukes** snorted. "Figures. If you're gonna hide a secret world, might as well make it pretty."

At the back of the group, **Erik Lehnsherr** said nothing.

He wasn't looking at the garden.

He was looking at the house, measuring it. What it took to build something like this. What it took to _keep_ it hidden.

And what kind of man planned this far ahead.

He glanced once at **Charles Xavier,** walking calmly beside him.

_Curious_, Erik realized. Not impressed. Curious.

### **Inside**

The mansion smelled like wood polish and old books. Lived in. Not a facility. Not a bunker.

Charles and **Hank McCoy** split naturally, guiding people through the halls.

"No locks," Charles said as he showed one of the rooms. "If you need space, take it."

Hank lingered a moment longer with **Morph**, who stood in the doorway staring at his reflection in a mirror that didn't distort.

"If anything feels… unstable," Hank said gently, "come find me."

Morph nodded. Then he frowned. Then nodded again; like he wasn't sure which response belonged to him.

Down the hall, **Raven Darkhölme** watched everything without comment. She clocked exits. Cameras. Lines of sight. When a maid passed, Raven's posture shifted just slightly; blending, already.

Blob dropped his bag on a bed and didn't unpack.

"Not staying long," he muttered to no one in particular.

Erik waited until the house settled.

"Why bring them all here?" He asked Charles quietly, near a window overlooking the ground.

Charles didn't answer right away. "Because tonight they will have to choose something after they hear me," he said. "It's about truth."

"And after tonight?"

Charles met his eyes. "Then everyone decides for themselves."

Erik considered that.

"Tonight," Charles added, "we talk."

### **The Lab**

The lab wasn't hidden so much as it contained. Glass walls. Soft lighting. Screens dormant until Charles activated them.

They gathered loosely; not seated, not formal.

Victor Creed arrived at last.

**Creed** had somehow found a bottle of beer and a cigar. He leaned against a counter like he owned the place, smoke curling lazily toward the ceiling.

"No offense, Professor," he said, cracking the bottle open, "but this feels like a lecture."

Several people were stiffened.

Raven's eyes flicked at Charles. Curious how he'd handle it.

Charles didn't react.

"This isn't a lecture," he said. "It's context."

He brought up a map. Then another. Financial trails. Front companies. Conflicts that looked unrelated until they didn't.

"There is a man," Charles continued, "who has spent decades manipulating instability. Wars, revolutions, and social fractures. He funds research into human evolution while publicly condemning it. He doesn't hate mutants."

A name appeared.

**Sebastian Shaw**

"He sees us as leverage."

Mimic leaned forward. "He did the camp?"

"He funded the people who funded it," Charles replied. "He prefers distance."

Victor laughed. "Smart."

More images appeared; camps, labs, proxy conflicts.

Blob shifted uncomfortably. "So, we're data points."

"Yes," Charles said simply.

Erik's eyes narrowed as one image lingered.

"That," he said sharply, pointing at the screen as an old military report surfaced. "That operation. Vornavia."

The room was still.

Charles nodded slowly. "Yes. Shaw orchestrated it through misinformation. Command interference. Deliberate breakdown of communication."

Erik's voice dropped. "That was my home."

The room went quiet.

Morph's hands clenched.

Mimic looked at Erik, really looked at him, like pieces were snapping into place.

Raven's jaw tightened, just slightly.

Charles moved closer, placing a hand on Erik's shoulder.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I didn't know."

Erik shook his head. "No. He will be sorry."

Anger sharpened his movements as he turned away.

"Running already?" Victor said around his cigar. "Figures."

He turned to leave; fury contained but unmistakable.

Before he reached the door-

_"Erik."_

The voice was not spoken aloud. It was in Erik's mind.

Erik stopped. Slowly turned back.

"You said you don't read minds without consent," Erik said coldly.

"I don't," Charles replied, and voiced steadily. "I just need you to listen."

A pause.

"Why do you think we've done all of this in the first place?"

Erik's breathing slowed.

He returned to the table.

"There are more camps," Charles continued. "More people like us. We can free them. We can dismantle Shaw's influence."

"And then what?" Blob asked. "You make us heroes?"

"No," Charles said. "We give people a choice."

"Guide them?" Victor scoffed. "You mean tell them what's good for them."

Charles met his gaze. "I mean remind them they're not alone."

Victor took a long drag, then shrugged. "Not my kind of dream."

He turned to leave.

The lab emptied slowly.

Victor Creed was the first to go. He didn't argue. Didn't linger. The cigar was gone; the beer bottle left behind like an afterthought. He laughed once on his way out, short, sharp, and disappeared down the corridor without looking back.

Jason and the unnamed mutants followed in quieter ways. No confrontation. No speeches. Just exhaustion. A need to be anywhere that wasn't part of a plan.

No one tried to stop them.

Charles watched them leave; hands folded behind his back.

"If you need time," he said to those who remained, "take it. There's no rush."

No one answered.

The screens were dimmed. The **X** faded into black.

Erik stayed where he was.

### **Night**

The mansion settled into silence.

Erik stood at the window of his room; the ground below washed in moonlight. He didn't move. Didn't sit. His reflection stared back at him, thin and tired.

Vornavia came back to him in fragments.

Sirens.

Fire.

The sound of metal screaming as it twisted under his control for the first time.

The knowledge, clear even then, that someone had _allowed_ it to happen.

Shaw's face lingered on the screen in his mind. Not as a villain. As a _constant_.

Erik clenched his fists.

If Shaw continued, more places would burn. More camps would fill. More children would disappear into rooms with white walls and polite voices.

Across the mansion, others lay awake.

**Raven Darkhölme** sat on the edge of her bed; cataloging exits she didn't intend to use. She had seen men like Shaw before. Men who hid behind systems. Charles was different. That difference mattered.

**Fred Dukes** stared at the ceiling; arms folded over his chest. He didn't believe in dreams. But he believed in fewer cages.

**Morph** caught his reflection in the mirror again, and this time it didn't turn away. The face looking back was tiring. But it was still _there_.

**Calvin Rankin** sat cross-legged on the floor, palms pressed against his knees, trying to feel where his own thoughts ended, and others didn't begin. For the first time, the noise was quieter.

No one slept.

### **Morning**

Sunlight filtered through the tall windows as **Charles Xavier** and **Hank McCoy** walked toward the lab.

Neither spoke.

They didn't expect answers today.

"Charles."

Erik's voice stopped them.

He stood at the far end of the hall, dressed simply, expression composed. Behind him, Raven leaned against the wall. Blob stood with his hands in his pockets. Morph hovered uncertainly near the doorway. Mimic followed last, quiet but present.

Erik walked forward.

"You don't just want to finish our enemy," Erik said. "You want to stop what makes men like him possible."

Charles listened.

"You saw further than the rest of us," Erik continued. "Not just the pain, but what comes after it if no one intervenes."

Erik paused, choosing his words carefully.

"I don't trust the world," he said. "But I trust that this…" He gestured around them. "This might prevent it from breaking more people."

He raised his hand; not demanding, not dramatic.

"We'll help you stop him."

Raven nodded once.

Blob exhaled. "I'm not good at speeches," he muttered. "But yeah."

Morph stepped forward, uncertain, then steadied. Mimic followed his lead.

Charles smiled, not triumphant. Relieved.

"I knew you'd come to your own conclusion," he said.

He took Erik's hand.

The agreement wasn't sealed with words.

It didn't need to be.

## Chapter 4: Preparation

The lab felt different in daylight.

Less like a secret and more like a workshop, half-finished ideas, chalkboards filled with equations that cross disciplines; cables routed carefully but not neatly. This wasn't a weapon room. It was a place where thought had been allowed to grow without knowing what it would become.

At the center of the room stood the machine.

It wasn't large yet. Not imposing. A circular platform, copper and ceramic interwoven, conduits spiraling upward into a helmet suspended by a delicate frame. The metal looked almost organic, as if it had learned to bend rather than resist.

Erik walked around slowly.

"This is what you've been building," he said. Not a question.

Charles nodded. "Trying to."

Hank adjusted a panel on the side, fingers moving with familiarity. "Prototype is the right word. It's incomplete. Temperamental. And absolutely not ready for extended use."

Erik glanced at him. "That usually means you're close."

Hank smiled despite himself.

Charles stepped closer to the platform. "The idea is simple," he said. "The execution is not." He gestured to the helmet. "Mutant minds emit patterns, distinct neurological signatures. Cerebro amplifies my telepathic reach and translates those patterns into something we can observe."

"And why build it?" Raven asked from the doorway.

Charles didn't answer immediately.

"So we're not blind," he said finally. "So we don't find out about people like us after they've already been hurt."

Morph stood near the back, arms folded tightly. Mimic hovered closer to Erik than he realized. Blob leaned against a reinforced column, skeptical but listening.

Charles took a breath. "We'll start small."

He seated himself carefully, allowing Hank to lower the helmet into place. The machine hummed softly, not aggressively, but attentively, like it was waiting.

Hank nodded. "I'm bringing power up slowly."

The room dimmed.

Then;

Light.

Sparse. Erratic. Sharp.

Psychically.

### Grey's Residence

**Jean Grey**.

This wasn't a plan.

Hope, not as an idea.

**Lucas Bishop**.

## Chapter 6: The Cost of Interference

Sebastian Shaw smiled easily for the cameras.

He always did.

He didn't have to.

Real.

## Chapter 7: War

Red smoke. A ripple in space.

**Azazel** appears behind them.

Mystique freezes.

"Hello, dear."

Not yet.

Mutants at the top because we can endure what humans can't."

Not a plea.

A correction.

Metal screams.

"You asked for this."

Not traps. **Protocols**.

### Raven vs Azazel: Movement vs Memory

**Mystique** recovers first; instinctively shifting, disappearing into color and shadow.

Then space bends.

- Azazel never stays still
- Raven never stays the same
- Every strike missed by inches
- Every teleport left behind a memory they don't talk about

Azazel smiles when he fights with her. That's cruelty.

"You always were hard to catch."

This fight isn't about winning.

It's about **keeping Raven from helping anyone else**.

And it works.

### Mimic vs Creed: Adaptation vs Appetite

Somewhere lower in the house, steel bends inward as **Sabretooth** lands hard' already grinning.

Across from him, **Mimic** is shaking; not with fear, but overload.

Mimic copies:

- Strength
- Reflexes
- Healing

But Creed doesn't fight to win fast.

He fights to **exhaust**.

- Every blow forced Mimic to adapt again
- Every adaptation cost stability
- Every second makes Mimic less himself

By the time Mimic realizes what's happening, he's already burning out.

Creed laughs.

"You don't get to be me for free."

### Morph vs Frost: Identity vs Intrusion

Elsewhere, mirrors slide out of the walls.

**Morph** steps into a room that reflects him back, as everyone he's ever been.

And waiting calmly in the center is **Emma Frost**.

She doesn't attack.

She observes.

Every time Morph changes, she smiles, because she already knows who he _wants_ to be.

"Do you ever wonder," she asks gently,

"If there's anything underneath all this?"

Morph tries to outthink her.

That's the mistake.

This isn't combat.

It's **a containment**.

### Erik vs Shaw: Power meets Preparation

At the heart of the house is Shaw's room.

Not a throne room.

A **battery**.

Walls lined with conductive alloys.

Floor plates humming with stored energy.

Every surface designed to _feed_ him.

**Magneto** enters with **Banshee** and **Bishop** backing him.

They attack together.

- Banshee's scream distorts the air
- Bishop absorbs the recoil, redirects it
- Erik tears the room apart piece by piece

For a moment; it works.

Then Shaw steps forward.

**Sebastian Shaw** absorbs:

- The sound
- The energy
- The impact

He _likes_ this.

With a single release, he:

- Slams Banshee into a wall; hard enough to silence him
- Overloads Bishop; forcing him to dump energy just to stay standing

Shaw looks at Erik again.

"You brought friends," he says.

"I brought infrastructure."

The room hums louder.

And Erik realizes something chilling:

This place wasn't built to stop mutants.

It was built to **harvest them**.

### **The moment hope starts slipping**

The first thing Erik notices isn't pain.

It's **silence**.

Banshee's scream fades into a rasp, his throat burned raw by feedback he can't control anymore. Bishop drops to one knee, glowing veins flickering as the room drains him faster than he can dump the energy.

Shaw hasn't moved.

**Sebastian Shaw** takes a slow breath, and the room answers; lights dim, floor plates glow brighter, walls thrum like a living thing.

"This is why you lose," Shaw says calmly.

"You fight systems like they're people."

He releases another wave.

Bishop is thrown back, hard, not unconscious, but _done_. His body refuses to cooperate. Banshee doesn't get up at all.

Erik is alone again.

### **The house turns on the others**

Elsewhere, the cracks widen.

### Raven vs Azazel: Fatigue sets in

**Mystique** lands awkwardly, her disguise slipping for half a second; long enough for Azazel to notice.

**Azazel** stops smiling.

Now he's serious.

He doesn't taunt anymore. He teleports her _into walls_, into ceilings, into narrow spaces where she can't shift fast enough. Every escape costs her breath. Every breath costs her time.

She's still fighting, but she's losing ground.

### Mimic vs Creed: the body gives out

**Mimic** staggers.

Too many powers.

Too many systems conflict each other.

**Sabretooth** smells it immediately.

He doesn't rush. He circles.

One clean hit sends Mimic through a reinforced wall. Healing factor copied but imperfect. Slower. Sloppier.

Creed leans in close.

"You're empty."

Mimic tries to stand. His legs don't listen to him.

### Morph vs Frost: the quiet defeat

**Morph** shifts again; Erik, Raven, someone stronger, but it's wrong. Emma already broke the rhythm.

**Emma Frost** doesn't raise her voice.

She just steps forward.

"You don't know who you are," she says softly.

"So how could you ever beat me?"

Morph freezes; not because she stops him, but because he hesitates.

That's enough.

The doors seal.

### **Back to Erik: And the truth lands**

Erik tears the floor up, hurling metal like artillery. Shaw absorbs it all; not straining, not flinching.

Then Shaw gestures.

Panels slide open.

Behind reinforced glass: **mutants**.

Hooked into machines. Monitored. Studied. Preserved.

Not prisoners screaming for help.

Assets waiting their turn.

Shaw watches Erik's face change; not rage, not grief.

**Recognition.**

"I saved them," Shaw says.

"From extinction. From weaknesses. From the lie that peace was ever an option."

Erik's control wavers; just a fraction.

The room punishes him for it.

A focused blast sends him crashing through a pillar. He hits the ground hard, helmet rolling away for a moment before snapping back into place.

Shaw stands over to him.

"You came to stop me."

"But look around, Erik."

The house hums.

The fights echo faintly, distant, _losing_.

"You're already too late."

### **The stillness before everything breaks**

Erik is on one knee.

The house is screaming around him; machines feeding Shaw, walls humming, metal groaning under strain.

**Sebastian Shaw** thinks he's won.

Then Erik remembers something **Professor X** once said, quietly, almost casually:

_"Rage lets you lift mountains._

_But you can't hold them there."_

_"You need something between rage and serenity."_

Erik exhales.

Not in anger.

Not in grief.

He closes his eyes.

For the first time in this house, there is **focus**.

### **The house answers the wrong man**

Shaw feels it first.

Not an attack, a resonance.

The walls vibrate.

The floor plates lose rhythm.

Energy conduits overload not outward, but _inward_.

"What are you doing?" Shaw asks; and this time, it's not rhetorical.

The entire property begins to **come apart at the seams**.

Metal doesn't explode.

It **listens**.

### **Awakening made visible**

Beams tear free without sound.

Panels peel away like skin.

Weapons, wiring, steel frames, all rise.

Erik lifts into the air, suspended in a slow orbit of rotating metal; not chaotic, not violent. **Precise**.

He's careful.

The humans in the house aren't touched.

Debris bends _around_ them.

This isn't rage.

This is **judgment**.

### **The balance shifts everywhere**

Everyone feels it.

- Raven plants her feet; times Azazel's teleport, and _catches him_ mid-reentry
- Mimic stabilizes, copies _only what he needs_, and drops Creed with his own momentum
- Morph stops shifting, commits to one form and breaks Frost's control
- Banshee finds his voice again
- Bishop absorbs cleanly, redirects deliberately

They don't fight harder.

They fight **smarter;** lessons learned in blood, finally applied.

Shaw's house, his system, is gone.

### **The end of Shaw**

Erik descends.

The metal settles behind him, obedient, waiting.

Shaw stands in front of him, still powerful, but **too late**.

Erik looks him in the eyes.

No speech.

No warning.

Steel bars; surgical, saline-white, drive through Shaw's body in a blink.

Four. Five.

No time to absorb.

No time to react.

Blood spills from Shaw's mouth as disbelief finally reaches him.

Erik lowers the metal to the ground.

Walks forward.

Shaw takes his last breath.

Erik says **nothing**.

### **Inheritance, not victory**

He bends down.

Takes the helmet.

Turns his back on Shaw.

When Erik puts it on, it isn't armor.

It's a **line drawn**.

He looks at the team; bruised, standing, victorious in their own battles.

They don't cheer.

They understand.

This wasn't the end of a war.

It was the birth of something inevitable.

And when Erik Lehnsherr stands there, helmet on, metal silent around him;

you know this truth:

He didn't become Shaw.

He became **something Shaw never was**.

## Chapter 8: What Remains

Morning comes quietly.

Too quiet.

**Professor X** wakes up to the soft rhythm of machines; oxygen mask on his face, monitors, sterile light. For a moment, he didn't move. When he does, it's slow and deliberate. He removes the mask himself, as if refusing to be kept asleep any longer.

The hallway outside is empty.

Then footsteps.

**Hank McCoy** stands there, holding a newspaper. He doesn't speak. He doesn't need to.

Charles takes it.

The headline is merciless.

_Sebastian Shaw killed; witnesses claim mutant attack._

No nuance.

No context.

No truth.

Just blame.

Something heavy settles in Charles's chest; not shock, not anger, **grief without tears**.

Hank finally speaks, and voice low.

"I'm sorry, Charles.

I tried to stop them."

Charles nods faintly, as if the words passed straight through him.

They sit together in the hall; the place that once echoed with plans, ideals, optimism.

Charles tried to sense Erik, but he couldn't, due to the helmet.

Hank continues; each sentence is another fracture.

"Everything we tried to build… it's gone."

"Erik took the captives. Some of our own went with him."

A pause.

"Morph, Mimic, Bishop, Cassidy; they refused."

"They didn't return either. They're hiding now. After what happened… they don't have a choice."

Hank looks at Charles, finally asking the question he's been avoiding.

"What do we do now?"

Charles doesn't answer immediately.

His face tightens; not with anger, but with the quiet devastation of someone who understands **why** things failed… and that understanding hurts more than ignorance ever could.

It looks like he might cry.

Then; a thought.

Jean.

Charles stands and walks to a familiar doorway.

Inside, **Jean Grey** is studying. Books open. Focused. Unburdened by the weight of yesterday; not because she doesn't care, but because she hasn't been broken by it yet.

Charles watches her for a long moment.

Then he speaks; gently.

"Hope isn't gone, Hank."

Hank looks at him, uncertain.

Charles turns; resolve returning, not loud, not triumphant, **earned**.

"We couldn't change the minds of adults whose hearts were hardened by their past."

"But we can give the young a chance, before fear becomes habit."

"Before power becomes identity."

"Before conflict becomes all they see."

He looks back at Jean.

"We teach them to see people… not enemies."

A beat.

"We start again."

Hank exhales; not relief, but acceptance.

"A school?"

Charles nodded.

Not a fortress.

Not an army.

A place to **learn**.

A place to **belong**.

### **Closing image**

Outside, the world is already changing.

Somewhere beyond reach, **Magneto** is leading survivors into the unknown; not as a villain, not yet as a tyrant, but as a man who chose certainty over hope.

Inside these walls, Charles chooses something harder.

**Faith.**

The camera lingers on the empty halls; soon to be filled again.

Children running.

Questions asked.

Mistakes made.

Not peace.

But **possibility**.

And that is where the story ends.

Not with victory.

But beginning.

### 1997

Far from the noise of the world, inside a stone chamber that feels more like a sanctuary than a throne room, **Magneto** sits with his children.

**Pietro Maximoff** is restless, questions tumbling out faster than he can sit still.

**Wanda Maximoff** listens quietly, absorbing every word.

Erik is calm here. Softer.

He tells them a story; not of wars, not of victories, but of a man who once saved him.

He didn't say the name at first.

"We disagreed," Erik says, almost smiling.

"About the world. About people. About how to protect what we love."

The children ask why they stayed apart.

Erik answers honestly.

"Because sometimes love doesn't mean walking the same path."

"It means believing the other will do good; even when you cannot follow."

Wanda asks the question that matters most.

"If you left… why does he still matter?"

Erik's smile lingers, bittersweet.

"Because without him," he says softly,

"none of this would exist."

He looks out the window.

### **The world he built**

The camera pulls back.

Below the castle, a nation breathes.

People from different cultures.

Different languages.

Different histories.

Mutants who arrived as refugees, now living as citizens.

Markets, homes, and children play freely in the open.

No banners of conquest.

No walls of fear.

Just belonging.

This is **Genosha**.

A country protected not by domination, but by resolve, and by a promise that what happened elsewhere will never happen here again.

Erik watches his children, then the city, then the horizon.

Distance still exists.

Differences remain.

But something unseen; something unbroken, still connects him to a man far away in another school, teaching another generation to hope.

The story doesn't end with agreement.

It ends with **respect**.

And with a world, imperfect, fragile, that exists because two men once believed mutants deserved a future.

_Fade out._
