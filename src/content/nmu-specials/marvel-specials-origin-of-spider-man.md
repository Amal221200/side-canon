# Marvel Specials – Origin of Spider-Man

## Chapter One: Chosen

Queens, New York.

Rain had a way of making ordinary days feel heavier.

Benjamin Parker looked up from the old pickup in his driveway as a black sedan rolled slowly to a stop outside the house. Two men stepped out, both dressed in dark suits, carrying leather briefcases that looked too expensive for the neighborhood.

May noticed them through the kitchen window.

"Ben?"

He wiped the grease from his hands with a rag before walking toward the front door.

The men waited politely beneath the porch roof, rain tapping softly against polished shoes.

"Mr. and Mrs. Parker?"

Ben nodded.

"I'm Benjamin Parker."

The older of the two removed his glasses.

"My name is Daniel Foster. I'm an attorney representing Richard and Mary Parker."

Ben smiled instinctively.

"My brother finally remembered we exist?"

The attorney didn't smile back.

Something inside Ben tightened.

"I'm afraid we've come regarding their estate."

Silence.

Not the comfortable kind.

The kind that arrives before the world changes.

May stepped closer.

"...Estate?"

The attorney lowered his eyes.

"Richard and Mary Parker disappeared three weeks ago during an overseas assignment. After extensive investigation, federal authorities have concluded that the likelihood of their survival is... extremely low."

The words hung in the air.

Ben heard them.

He simply refused to believe them.

"No."

His voice came out smaller than he intended.

"There has to be some mistake."

"I'm very sorry."

The attorney opened the briefcase and carefully placed several documents onto the dining table.

"Their will."

Another envelope rested beside it.

"And a personal letter."

Neither Ben nor May reached for it immediately.

The rain filled the silence instead.

Eventually, May unfolded the letter with trembling hands.

Richard's handwriting.

Unmistakable.

---

*Ben.*

*If you're reading this, then Mary and I didn't make it home.*

*I wish I could explain everything. The truth is... there are people involved who would use our family against us. We couldn't risk Peter becoming one more target.*

*We made the hardest decision of our lives.*

*Peter has been placed safely under another name at Saint Gabriel Children's Home until this situation is resolved.*

*If you're reading this, then it never was.*

*Please don't think we stopped loving him.*

*Every choice we made was because we loved him.*

*Raise him better than I ever could.*

*Tell him who we were.*

*Tell him we never stopped coming home.*

*Your brother.*

*Richard.*

---

May lowered the letter slowly.

Her eyes were already wet.

Ben stared at the page for several long seconds before anger finally found him.

"You idiot..."

Not hatred.

Heartbreak.

"You absolute idiot."

He slammed a hand onto the table.

"You should've come to us!"

The attorneys remained silent.

Ben shook his head.

"You trusted strangers with your son?"

"He believed he was protecting him," the attorney said quietly.

"I don't care what he believed."

Ben grabbed his coat from the chair.

"Where is he?"

The attorney slid another document across the table.

"Saint Gabriel Children's Home."

Ben was already heading toward the door.

May followed without hesitation.

"We're bringing him home."

### Saint Gabriel Children's Home

The building smelled faintly of disinfectant and baby powder.

Children laughed somewhere down the hallway.

Life continuing as though nothing had happened.

Ben couldn't understand how.

A woman in her sixties greeted them gently.

"You must be Mr. and Mrs. Parker."

Ben nodded.

"We're here for Peter."

She studied them for only a moment before smiling softly.

"He has your brother's eyes."

She led them through quiet hallways lined with small cribs.

Some babies slept.

Some cried.

Others simply stared upward at ceilings they were too young to understand.

At the very end of the room...

She stopped.

"There."

A tiny crib.

Inside lay an infant wrapped in a pale blue blanket.

Fast asleep.

No idea the world had already taken everything from him.

May approached first.

Slowly.

Almost afraid to disturb the moment.

She reached down and lifted him into her arms.

Peter stirred.

His eyes opened only halfway before settling on the unfamiliar faces above him.

He didn't cry.

He simply looked.

Curious.

Quiet.

Ben stepped closer.

For a moment he saw Richard.

Not the baby.

His brother.

The way he smiled.

The stubbornness in his eyes.

Everything that should have still been there.

Peter reached out clumsily.

His tiny hand wrapped around Ben's finger.

It wasn't a strong grip.

Barely more than instinct.

But it was enough.

Ben looked down at the child.

The tears came before he realized they had.

He laughed once through them.

Softly.

"We're taking him home."

The woman nodded.

"I thought you might."

---

The drive back to Queens was quiet.

Peter slept peacefully in May's arms as rain slid across the windshield.

Neither of them spoke for several miles.

Finally Ben glanced toward the back seat.

"So small."

May smiled through tired eyes.

"He won't remember today."

"No."

Ben tightened his hands around the steering wheel.

"But we'll remember it for him."

Another long silence.

Ben looked once more into the rear-view mirror.

Richard always wanted to protect him.

He just forgot something important.

Family doesn't protect each other by staying apart.

They do it by staying together.

Ben smiled sadly.

"Welcome home, Peter."

Outside, the rain finally began to ease.

Neither of them noticed.

For the first time since Richard and Mary disappeared...

Peter Parker wasn't alone.

---

## Chapter Two: Best Friends

### Summer, 2007

Queens had a way of announcing new neighbors.

Not with welcome signs.

With moving trucks.

Benjamin Parker tossed another baseball into the air.

"Eyes on the ball, Pete."

Five-year-old Peter narrowed his eyes with complete determination.

The baseball drifted toward him.

He swung.

Missed.

The bat spun out of his hands instead.

Ben laughed.

"Well... you definitely hit something."

Peter looked at the bat lying in the grass.

"I almost got it."

"You'll get it next time."

Peter picked the bat back up, tongue sticking out slightly as he concentrated.

Ben tossed another pitch.

This time Peter connected.

The ball bounced weakly across the lawn.

Peter's face lit up.

"I DID IT!"

"You sure did."

May watched from the porch, smiling into her glass of iced tea.

Before Ben could throw another ball, the sound of a truck's reverse alarm echoed down the quiet street.

A large moving truck slowly backed into the driveway of the house next door.

Peter immediately forgot baseball existed.

"New neighbors?"

Ben shaded his eyes.

"Looks like it."

The driver's door opened first.

A cheerful man climbed out, followed by a woman carrying a cardboard box.

Then...

A little girl with bright red hair jumped out of the passenger side before either parent could stop her.

She looked around the neighborhood with wide green eyes.

Peter stared.

She stared back.

Neither of them moved.

The adults did.

The man walked across the lawn with an easy smile.

"Afternoon. I'm Jonathan Watson."

He extended his hand.

Ben shook it firmly.

"Ben Parker."

"My wife, Madeline."

May stepped down from the porch.

"I'm May. Welcome to the neighborhood."

"We appreciate it," Madeline said. "Just moved in from the other side of Queens."

Jonathan chuckled.

"Closer to Midtown Elementary. Figured the shorter commute would make mornings easier."

May smiled.

"Peter goes there too."

Jonathan looked toward the children.

"So does our daughter."

He turned.

"Mary Jane."

The little girl walked over without hesitation.

She stopped in front of Peter.

"I'm Mary Jane Watson."

She held out her hand exactly the way she'd seen the adults do.

Peter looked at it for a second before awkwardly shaking it.

"I'm Peter Parker."

She smiled.

"Wanna play?"

Peter looked toward Ben.

Ben shrugged.

"Looks like baseball practice is over."

Peter didn't need to be told twice.

The two children immediately ran toward the baseball resting in the grass.

Mary Jane picked it up.

"How do you play?"

Peter proudly grabbed the bat.

"I'll teach you."

Ben watched them disappear into the backyard together.

May slipped her hand into his.

"They became friends awfully fast."

Ben smiled.

"Kids don't waste time."

---

Friendship, Peter would later learn, wasn't something that happened all at once.

It happened a thousand little times.

Building a treehouse behind the Parker house with Ben handing them wooden planks while pretending not to notice they hammered more fingers than nails.

Walking to school every morning because the Watsons' house sat only a few doors away.

Watching Saturday morning cartoons while bowls of cereal slowly turned soggy on the coffee table.

Summer afternoons spent chasing fireflies until May called both of them home for dinner.

Trading Pokémon cards and arguing over whose collection was better.

Learning to ride bicycles.

Racing scooters down the sidewalk.

Halloween costumes.

Snowball fights.

Paper airplanes thrown from the treehouse.

Entire afternoons that felt like they would last forever.

Somehow...

Peter couldn't remember a time before Mary Jane.

And he couldn't imagine one after.

---

Midtown Elementary became their second home.

Peter loved asking questions.

Sometimes too many.

Mrs. Collins would finish explaining one science lesson before Peter's hand was already in the air again.

"What if..."

"How..."

"Why..."

The class laughed.

Mrs. Collins never did.

"Excellent question, Peter."

Mary Jane always sat nearby.

Sometimes because the seating chart allowed it.

Sometimes because she quietly traded seats with someone else.

Peter never noticed.

She did.

---

Not everyone liked Peter Parker.

Flash Thompson noticed him during first grade.

Peter was smaller.

Quieter.

Easy to embarrass.

Flash wasn't cruel all the time.

Only when people were watching.

One afternoon during recess, Peter reached into his pocket.

Empty.

"My lunch money..."

Flash spun a dollar coin around his fingers.

"Lose something, Parker?"

Peter frowned.

"That's mine."

Flash smirked.

"You got proof?"

Several kids laughed.

Peter looked down instead.

Then another voice interrupted.

"Give it back."

Mary Jane stood beside Peter with her hands on her hips.

Flash rolled his eyes.

"Or what?"

"I'll tell Mrs. Collins."

Flash scoffed.

"Tattletale."

He flicked the coin toward Peter.

It landed in the dirt.

Peter picked it up.

"...Thanks."

Mary Jane shrugged.

"You would've done the same."

Peter nodded.

He would have.

Without thinking.

---

Flash Thompson was also the best athlete in school.

Fastest runner.

Strongest throw.

First pick for every baseball game.

Teachers praised him.

Students admired him.

Peter did too.

Even after everything.

One afternoon during Sports Day, Flash launched a baseball almost to the edge of the field.

The entire class cheered.

Peter clapped quietly with everyone else.

Flash noticed.

For just a second...

He looked like he might smile.

Then another boy shouted his name.

Flash shoved the feeling away.

"Come on!"

He ran back onto the field.

Peter never understood him.

Not yet.

---

Years passed the way childhood always does.

Slowly while living it.

Too quickly when remembering it.

The treehouse grew smaller.

The bicycles became too short.

The Pokémon cards disappeared into desk drawers.

Homework became harder.

Science fairs replaced finger painting.

The questions Peter asked became more complicated.

The friendship never did.

---

### Spring, 2013.

The final day of elementary school.

Cardboard boxes sat beside every desk.

Mrs. Collins smiled sadly as students emptied years of memories into backpacks.

Outside, children ran toward waiting parents, celebrating the beginning of summer.

Peter and Mary Jane sat together beneath the old oak tree outside the school.

Neither of them seemed in a hurry to leave.

"They say middle school's different," Mary Jane said.

Peter shrugged.

"Probably."

"They say everyone changes."

Peter looked at her.

"You think we will?"

She thought about it.

Then smiled.

"No."

Peter smiled back.

"Me neither."

She held out her hand.

Not for a handshake this time.

A pinky.

"Best friends?"

Peter hooked his little finger around hers.

"Best friends."

Neither of them knew that life would keep testing that promise.

Or how many times they would find their way back to it.

For now...

They stood together and walked home the same way they always had.

Side by side.

Without needing to say another word.

---

## Chapter Three: New Beginnings

### Autumn, 2013

Middle school felt enormous.

The hallways were longer.

The lockers were taller.

The teachers expected more.

The students tried harder to look older than they really were.

Peter Parker still wasn't convinced anyone had actually grown up.

Some things changed.

Some things never did.

Peter was still the first student to raise his hand.

Mary Jane still waited for him every morning outside the school gates.

Flash Thompson was still the most popular boy in school.

And Peter was still his favorite target.

---

The morning before school began, Ben rolled an old bicycle out of the garage.

It wasn't new.

The paint had faded years ago.

The handlebars showed scratches from another child's adventures long before Peter.

Ben rubbed the seat proudly.

"Took me two weekends to get her working again."

Peter's eyes widened.

"For me?"

Ben grinned.

"Middle school's farther away."

Peter ran over, wrapping his arms around Ben before even looking at the bicycle properly.

"It's perfect."

Ben laughed.

"I don't know about perfect."

"It is."

Peter meant it.

Later that afternoon, Ben jogged behind him as Peter wobbled down the sidewalk.

"Don't look at the wheel!"

"I'm trying!"

"Look where you want to go!"

Peter nearly rode into Mrs. Rodriguez's mailbox.

Ben caught the bicycle before it fell.

"Almost."

Peter laughed.

"I almost got it."

"You'll get it."

Just like baseball.

---

The first day of school arrived.

Peter and Mary Jane leaned their bicycles against the rack together.

"You nervous?" she asked.

Peter adjusted his backpack.

"A little."

"Me too."

They walked inside.

Everything smelled like fresh paint and unopened textbooks.

Different building.

Same nerves.

---

Flash Thompson had somehow grown six inches over the summer.

His voice was deeper.

His confidence even louder.

"Parker!"

Peter sighed before turning around.

Flash bounced a basketball once.

"Try not to get lost."

Mary Jane folded her arms.

"You spend a lot of time thinking about Peter."

Several nearby students laughed.

Flash rolled his eyes.

"Whatever."

He walked away.

Peter smiled.

"...Thanks."

"I know."

---

Their homeroom teacher stood at the front of the class.

"Before we begin..."

She smiled toward the doorway.

"We have a new student."

A boy stepped inside wearing an expensive navy blazer despite everyone else wearing ordinary hoodies.

He looked uncomfortable.

Not shy.

Just... unfamiliar.

"This is Harry Osborn."

The teacher gestured toward the empty desk.

"Please make him feel welcome."

Whispers spread immediately.

"His dad owns Oscorp."

"I heard they have a penthouse."

"That's so cool."

Flash frowned slightly.

For the first time in years...

Someone else had everyone's attention.

Harry quietly took his seat.

He wished he didn't.

---

Weeks passed.

Classes became routine.

Homework multiplied.

Friendships settled into place.

Peter remained at the top of every science quiz.

Mary Jane excelled in literature.

Flash dominated gym class.

Harry...

Kept mostly to himself.

---

The first algebra unit test arrived.

The room was silent except for pencils scratching paper.

Peter finished early.

He always did.

Harry did not.

---

The following afternoon, their teacher handed the tests back.

"There were some excellent scores."

She smiled toward Peter.

"Mr. Parker. Perfect score."

Peter lowered his eyes as several classmates looked his way.

The teacher continued.

"A few of you may need extra practice."

Her gaze shifted.

"Mr. Osborn."

Harry looked down at the large red mark across the top of his paper.

42%.

His shoulders sank.

---

Lunch was louder than usual.

Peter finished eating while Mary Jane talked with classmates about an upcoming history project.

He stood to throw away his tray.

That's when he noticed Harry.

Still sitting alone.

His sandwich untouched.

One page of algebra problems lay open beside his lunch tray.

Harry stared at the same equation as if willing it to solve itself.

Peter hesitated.

Then walked over.

"You skipped a step."

Harry looked up, startled.

"...What?"

Peter gently picked up the pencil lying beside the notebook.

"If you move this variable first..."

He wrote one line.

Then another.

Then another.

"The equation looks complicated because you're trying to solve everything at once."

Peter slid the notebook back.

"But algebra's really just solving one small problem at a time."

Harry blinked.

Peter pointed to each line.

"This tells you what changes."

"This tells you what stays the same."

"And once you know that..."

He circled the answer.

"...the rest is easy."

Harry stared at the page.

For the first time all week...

It actually made sense.

He looked up.

"I..."

Peter was already standing.

"You'll get it."

Harry finally found his voice.

"Thanks."

Peter smiled politely.

"No problem."

He picked up his tray and walked away.

Harry watched him disappear into the crowd.

He looked back down at the notebook.

Then quietly solved the next equation by himself.

This time...

He got the answer right.

---

## Chapter Four: An Unexpected Friend

### Autumn, 2013

Rain had a way of making New York feel slower.

The school halls, usually overflowing with students eager to escape, were nearly empty.

Only a handful remained.

Peter Parker stepped out through the front doors with his backpack slung over one shoulder, balancing his bicycle beside him.

Science Olympiad practice had run later than usual.

He didn't mind.

He'd spent the last two hours building a bridge from popsicle sticks capable of holding nearly twenty kilograms.

That counted as a good afternoon.

Thunder rolled somewhere above Queens.

Peter adjusted his glasses.

"...Probably should've left earlier."

He climbed onto his bicycle anyway.

---

The rain began gently.

By the second block...

It didn't.

Sheets of water hammered the streets.

Peter lowered his head and kept pedaling.

Almost home.

Just a little—

CLANK.

The pedals suddenly spun free.

The bicycle lurched sideways.

Peter barely had time to react.

He hit the pavement with a splash.

His backpack landed in a puddle beside him.

"...Ow."

He sat up slowly.

His glasses had slipped halfway off his face.

One corner of the right lens now carried a long crack.

Not enough to stop him seeing.

Enough to make everything frustratingly blurry.

Peter sighed.

"The chain..."

It had snapped completely free from the gears.

He rolled the bicycle beneath the awning of a closed convenience store and knelt beside it.

Rainwater dripped from his hair.

Grease coated his fingers.

The cracked lens made the chain appear doubled.

"...Come on..."

---

A black luxury sedan rolled quietly through the rain.

Inside, Harry Osborn stared absentmindedly through the rain-streaked window.

Across from him sat Norman Osborn, reading through a stack of reports.

He finally lowered one page.

"How was school?"

Harry shrugged.

"Pretty good."

"And algebra?"

Harry smiled slightly.

"I got an A."

Norman looked up.

"The tutoring helped?"

"I didn't get a tutor."

"No?"

"There was this kid..."

Harry searched for the name.

"Peter."

"He explained everything during lunch."

Norman nodded thoughtfully.

"Sometimes the best teachers aren't adults."

Harry looked back out the window.

Then suddenly leaned forward.

"Wait."

He pressed a hand against the glass.

"There!"

Norman followed his gaze.

A soaked teenager knelt beside a broken bicycle beneath a storefront awning.

Harry immediately recognized him.

"That's Peter."

The driver continued forward.

Harry spoke quickly.

"Pull over."

Norman glanced at him.

"Why?"

"That's the kid I told you about."

The sedan slowed.

"...Pull over."

Without another question, the driver eased the car beside the curb.

The rear window lowered smoothly.

"Peter!"

Peter looked up.

At first all he saw was an expensive car.

Then—

"Harry?"

Harry leaned out slightly.

"What happened?"

Peter held up the loose bicycle chain.

"...Mechanical failure."

Harry laughed.

"Need a ride?"

Peter shook his head instinctively.

"I'll figure it out."

Norman observed quietly.

The bicycle.

The cracked glasses.

The soaked schoolbag.

The boy trying very hard not to look embarrassed.

Harry opened the door.

"Come on."

Peter hesitated.

"My bike..."

"We'll put it in the trunk."

"I don't want to be any trouble."

Norman finally spoke.

"I insist."

His voice wasn't commanding.

Simply matter-of-fact.

"No one should be repairing a bicycle in this weather."

Peter looked at the rain.

Then at the chain.

Then back at Harry.

"...Okay."

---

The bicycle disappeared neatly into the trunk.

Peter climbed into the back seat.

Everything inside smelled faintly of leather.

He tried very hard not to drip on anything.

Harry smiled.

"Dad..."

"This is Peter Parker."

Peter straightened slightly.

"Nice to meet you, sir."

Norman offered a polite nod.

"Norman Osborn."

Peter smiled.

"I know."

Harry laughed.

"Everybody knows."

Norman looked toward Peter again.

"So..."

"You're the one who rescued my son's algebra grade."

Peter scratched the back of his neck.

"It wasn't really rescuing."

"He just skipped a step."

Harry interrupted.

"Like... fifteen steps."

Peter shrugged.

"It happens."

Norman smiled faintly.

"What events do you compete in?"

Peter blinked.

"You knew about Science Olympiad?"

"You left school this late."

"It seemed the most likely explanation."

Peter couldn't help smiling.

"Bridge building. Engineering design. Physics."

Norman's interest became genuine.

"And which one do you enjoy most?"

"Engineering."

"Why?"

Peter answered immediately.

"Because it's solving problems people haven't solved yet."

Norman nodded once.

"Good answer."

The conversation flowed naturally after that.

Science.

Baseball.

Comics.

Old movies.

Harry mostly listened.

He hadn't expected Peter to be this funny.

Or this easy to talk to.

---

By the time they reached Manhattan...

The rain had only grown heavier.

Norman looked outside.

"You won't be cycling home tonight."

Peter looked through the window.

"...Probably not."

"Stay with us."

Peter looked surprised.

"I couldn't."

"It's closer."

"I don't want to impose."

Before Norman could answer—

Harry leaned forward.

"I've got a PlayStation."

Peter looked over.

"And comics."

Peter blinked.

Harry grinned.

"Lots of comics."

"...Really?"

"And you can explain how half the gadgets actually work."

Peter laughed.

"...Okay."

Norman smiled quietly to himself.

The deal had already been made.

---

The Osborn residence occupied the upper floors of one of Manhattan's tallest residential towers.

Peter stepped out of the elevator.

Stopped.

And simply looked.

Floor-to-ceiling windows overlooked the entire city.

Bookshelves stretched across one wall.

Modern artwork lined another.

Everything was elegant.

Without feeling empty.

"...Wow."

Harry grinned.

"First time?"

Peter nodded honestly.

"I've never been this high above the city."

Norman removed his coat.

"Bernard."

A middle-aged butler appeared almost instantly.

"Please find something comfortable for Mr. Parker."

"Of course, sir."

Norman turned back toward Peter.

"And perhaps we should call your aunt and uncle."

Peter immediately gave him the number.

Within minutes, Norman had personally assured Ben and May that Peter was safe and would be home the following morning.

May thanked him repeatedly.

Ben simply said,

"Tell the boy not to worry about the bike."

Norman smiled.

"I believe that's already being handled."

---

That evening passed faster than either boy expected.

Video games.

Comic books scattered across the floor.

Homework spread across the coffee table.

Peter's notebooks had been ruined by the rain.

Harry disappeared briefly before returning with a stack of unused notebooks.

"My dad buys these every year."

Peter looked at the pile.

"There are like... ten."

"I only need three."

"You sure?"

Harry shrugged.

"You'll actually use them."

Peter smiled.

"...Thanks."

They finished homework together.

Argued over whether Undertaker could defeat John Cena.

Built ridiculous inventions out of spare LEGO pieces.

Laughed far louder than either expected.

From his study upstairs, Norman heard every bit of it.

He paused outside the partially open door.

Harry hadn't laughed like that in months.

Norman watched Peter enthusiastically explaining why a fictional jet engine wouldn't actually work.

Harry listened with complete fascination.

Norman smiled to himself.

Perhaps...

This friendship had arrived exactly when his son needed it.

---

### The Next Morning

Morning came bright and clear.

The storm had washed the city clean.

Peter thanked Bernard for breakfast before gathering his things.

He noticed a brand-new backpack and a pair of glasses waiting beside the front door.

"...Whose is that?"

Norman looked up from his coffee.

"Yours."

Peter frowned.

"I can't accept that."

"You can."

"My old one still works."

Norman glanced toward the ruined schoolbag drying beside the fireplace.

"No."

"It doesn't."

Before Peter could protest again—

Harry pulled him toward the elevator.

"Come on."

When they reached the lobby...

Peter stopped.

Leaning against the curb stood a bicycle.

Brand new.

Red and silver.

The exact same size as his old one.

Peter slowly looked toward Norman.

"...Sir..."

Norman placed one hand lightly on the handlebars.

"A scientist should have reliable equipment."

Peter swallowed.

"I don't know how to thank you."

Norman smiled warmly.

"You already have."

Peter looked confused.

Norman nodded toward Harry.

"My son hasn't smiled this much in quite some time."

Harry looked away, suddenly embarrassed.

Norman continued.

"Take good care of the bicycle."

Peter nodded.

"I will."

"And..."

Norman extended his hand.

"I hope you'll continue being Harry's friend."

Peter didn't hesitate.

He shook it firmly.

"I'd like that."

The sedan pulled away toward school.

Behind it, secured carefully inside the trunck, Peter Parker's new bicycle was placed.

He couldn't stop looking at it through the rear window.

Harry laughed.

"You know..."

Peter looked over.

"I think this might be the best rainy day I've ever had."

Harry grinned.

"Mine too."

Neither of them realized it then.

But the ride to school that morning was the beginning of a friendship that would shape both of their lives for years to come.

---

The school parking lot had never seen a car quite like the Osborn sedan.

Students slowed down.

Some openly stared.

The rear door opened.

Harry stepped out first.

Then Peter climbed out from the opposite side.

Immediately drawing confused looks.

"...Parker?"

"...He knows Harry Osborn?"

Peter barely noticed.

He was too busy staring at his new bicycle as the chauffeur carefully unloaded it from the trunck.

Harry smiled.

"Told you they'd fix everything."

Peter ran a hand across the handlebars.

"...It's still hard to believe."

Norman rolled down the window.

"Have a good day, boys."

"You too, sir," Peter replied immediately.

Norman smiled before the sedan disappeared into traffic.

Harry looked toward Peter.

"So..."

"You ready?"

Peter nodded.

"Yeah."

---

Peter had barely locked his bicycle when a familiar voice called out.

"There you are!"

Mary Jane jogged across the courtyard.

She stopped.

Looked at the bicycle.

Then at Harry.

Then back at Peter.

"...Okay."

"I definitely missed something."

Peter rubbed the back of his neck.

"It's... kind of a long story."

Harry stepped forward first.

"I'm Harry."

He offered his hand.

"Harry Osborn."

Mary Jane shook it confidently.

"Mary Jane Watson."

She glanced toward Peter.

"The famous algebra student?"

Harry laughed.

"So you've heard that story already."

Peter groaned.

"...Can we never call it that again?"

"No," they answered together.

Peter sighed dramatically.

"I walked right into that one."

Mary Jane smiled.

"You usually do."

Harry laughed harder.

For the first time in a long while...

The three of them walked into school together.

---

It happened naturally.

No one decided they were friends.

It simply...

Stayed that way.

Lunch became three trays instead of two.

Science projects became easier.

History assignments became louder.

Harry discovered Peter could explain almost anything.

Peter discovered Harry was much funnier than people assumed.

Mary Jane discovered neither of them could survive a group project without accidentally turning it into a debate.

Homework often ended with comic books.

Comic books usually ended with video games.

Video games almost always ended with someone reminding Peter to go home before May started worrying.

---

Flash Thompson noticed.

"You got bodyguards now, Parker?"

Peter looked up from his locker.

"No."

Flash nodded toward Harry.

"Rich kid following you everywhere."

Held his breath.

Held.

Minutes later—

"...Please work."

THWIP.

"...Okay."

"...Now what?"

Several stories.

Instant regret.

"OH NO!"

Too fast.

"WHOA!"

THWIP.

Fast.

Uncontrolled.

Terrified.

Another swing.

His hands trembled.

Slowly...

Homemade.

"I'm going downstairs."

She reached the doorway before turning back.

"And Peter?"

"Live a life that leaves as few regrets as possible."

"It's what Ben would've wanted."

She smiled softly.

"Call me if you need me."

Peter managed the smallest nod.

"...Okay, May."

The door closed quietly behind her.

---

Peter looked around the room.

The destruction no longer felt satisfying.

Only exhausting.

He knelt slowly.

Picked up a broken spring.

Then another.

One page at a time...

He began cleaning.

Stacking notebooks.

Gathering scattered screws.

Straightening bent sketches.

Eventually...

His hand brushed against a small tin box beneath the bed.

He paused.

The same box from his childhood.

Peter opened it carefully.

Inside—

Old baseball cards.

Marbles.

Photographs.

Trading cards.

Near the bottom...

The worn Captain America card.

Its corners had faded even more with age.

Peter smiled faintly.

He remembered Ben's voice.

*"He reminded people that heroes can be ordinary men who choose to do the right thing."*

Peter looked at the card for several long moments.

Then carefully slid it back into the box.

Closed the lid.

And returned to cleaning.

This time...

He wasn't cleaning up a broken room.

He was preparing to begin again.

---

## Chapter Nine: Friendly Neighborhood Spider-Man

Months passed.

Grief never truly disappeared.

It simply learned to live beside everything else.

Life slowly found a rhythm again.

Peter still woke early for school.

Still finished homework.

Still won Science Olympiad competitions.

Still walked home with Mary Jane and Harry whenever he could.

Some things had changed.

Some things never would.

---

One evening, Peter sat across from May at the kitchen table.

"I've been thinking."

May looked up from her coffee.

"That's usually how your projects begin."

Peter smiled faintly.

"I want to volunteer."

"At the Queens Community Foundation."

May looked surprised.

"They need help repairing equipment."

"They can't really pay much."

"I'm not doing it for money."

Peter looked down.

"I just..."

"I need something to keep my mind busy."

May understood immediately.

She reached across the table and gently squeezed his hand.

"I think Uncle Ben would've been proud."

Peter smiled quietly.

"So do I."

---

The foundation became more than volunteer work.

It became another classroom.

Peter repaired broken electronics.

Learned threading.

Learned sewing.

Learned how different fabrics stretched.

How they tore.

How they held together.

Every evening...

He returned home with another idea.

The web-shooters became lighter.

Smaller.

More reliable.

His mask fit better.

The gloves no longer slipped.

His suit slowly became something more than mismatched athletic clothes.

It still wasn't perfect.

Far from it.

The stitching wasn't straight.

The fabric wasn't ideal.

The lenses fogged occasionally.

But...

He had made it.

Every thread.

Every seam.

Every improvement.

With his own hands.

---

Saturday morning.

Bright skies.

Cool air.

Harry had invited him to spend the day at the Osborn residence.

Mary Jane wanted to see a new movie downtown.

Peter politely declined both.

"I've got work."

Neither questioned him anymore.

They knew healing took time.

---

The community foundation closed early that afternoon.

Peter waited until the building emptied.

Then quietly walked behind the storage garage.

He opened his backpack.

Red.

Blue.

Mask.

Gloves.

Web-shooters.

Piece by piece...

Peter disappeared.

Spider-Man stood where he once had.

He climbed quietly toward the rooftop.

The city stretched endlessly before him.

Thousands of buildings.

Millions of lives.

He looked toward the skyline.

A gentle breeze moved across the rooftops.

Peter smiled beneath the mask.

"...Watch over us, Uncle Ben."

He stepped forward.

And jumped.

---

The first swing felt different.

Not perfect.

But familiar.

He timed the release better.

Caught the next web earlier.

His landings became smoother.

Each building felt less like an obstacle...

And more like a path.

People noticed.

Phones appeared.

Someone pointed upward.

"Look!"

"What is that?"

A child laughed.

"It's Spider-Man!"

Someone began recording.

Another took photographs.

Spider-Man waved awkwardly mid-swing.

Almost missed the next web.

"...Still working on that."

---

Smoke.

Black.

Thick.

Rising several blocks away.

Peter's smile disappeared instantly.

He changed direction.

An apartment building burned fiercely.

Firefighters worked desperately below.

"There's still people inside!"

Without thinking—

Spider-Man crashed through a second-floor window.

Heat slammed into him.

Smoke swallowed the hallway.

He covered his mouth instinctively.

"Hello?"

No answer.

Then—

A cough.

He followed it.

An elderly couple remained trapped behind a fallen beam.

"It's okay."

"I've got you."

The man looked at the masked teenager.

"...Spider-Man?"

Peter smiled.

"...Yeah."

"I guess so."

He lifted the beam with both hands.

Escorted them outside.

Firefighters immediately took over.

One of them looked toward the masked teenager.

"You just saved their lives."

Peter nodded awkwardly.

"...Happy to help."

Then he was already gone.

---

Only two blocks later—

Construction workers shouted frantically.

The cables supporting a suspended bathtub had snapped loose.

It swung wildly above the street.

Below—

Two young children stood frozen.

Spider-sense.

Everything slowed.

Peter moved before thinking.

One web.

Another.

He landed beneath the falling bathtub.

Both hands reached upward.

The impact nearly drove him into the pavement.

His knees bent.

His arms shook.

For one terrifying second...

He thought he might fail.

Then—

The bathtub stopped.

Only inches above the children.

They slowly opened their eyes.

A masked figure smiled down at them.

"...You two okay?"

The younger child nodded.

"...Mister Spider-Man?"

Peter carefully lowered the bathtub onto the street.

The children immediately hugged him.

"Thank you!"

Peter laughed softly.

"You're welcome."

He gently patted one of them on the head.

"But next time..."

He pointed toward the construction site.

"...Keep your eyes open, okay?"

They nodded enthusiastically.

"We will!"

Construction workers rushed over.

One foreman looked horrified.

"I'm so sorry!"

"We got distracted!"

Spider-Man's expression became serious.

"You were lucky."

He looked around at everyone.

"People trust you to keep them safe."

"Please..."

"Don't let this happen again."

The workers nodded immediately.

"It won't."

Peter gave a small wave.

Then fired another web.

THWIP.

He disappeared into the skyline.

---

Across the street—

Flash Thompson stood outside a convenience store with two classmates.

All three watched Spider-Man swing away between the buildings.

One of Flash's friends laughed.

"That guy's insane."

Flash never looked away.

"...No."

A smile slowly appeared across his face.

"He's..."

"...the coolest person I've ever seen."

Far above the city—

Spider-Man continued swinging through the afternoon sky.

He wasn't perfect.

He still had everything to learn.

He would make mistakes.

He would fall.

He would get back up.

Again.

And again.

Because somewhere beyond the clouds...

Peter liked to believe Ben Parker was still watching.

And for the first time since that terrible night...

Peter no longer swung through New York to escape his grief.

He swung toward the future.

The same city that had taken everything from him...

Would become the city he spent the rest of his life protecting.

**The End.**
